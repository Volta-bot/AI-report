\section{Experimental Results on Benchmark Dataset}
The core of the empirical study focuses on validating the Federated Learning (FL) framework using the Multi-horizon LSTM model across two distinct urban environments: \textbf{PeMS-BAY} (California highway) and \textbf{METR-LA} (Los Angeles city streets). The results are categorized into convergence stability, temporal forecasting accuracy, and a comparative trade-off analysis.

\subsection{Convergence Analysis}
The efficiency of the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm was monitored over 50 communication rounds to ensure that distributed training can match the stability of centralized methods.

% --- Chèn hình ảnh
 \begin{figure}[htbp]
     \centering
     \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figure/pems-bay-fl_convergence_rounds.png}
     \caption{Loss function reduction over 50 communication rounds.}
     \label{fig:convergence}
 \end{figure}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rapid Global Learning:} During the first 10 rounds, the global model experienced a "warm-up" phase. For PeMS-BAY, the error dropped by \textbf{41.3\%}, while METR-LA saw a \textbf{43.9\%} reduction. This indicates the model quickly identifies fundamental traffic periodicities.
    \item \textbf{Local Feature Integration:} From rounds 11 to 30, the learning rate stabilized as the model began to reconcile non-IID data from different edge clients.
    \item \textbf{Final Convergence:} By the 50th round, the system achieved a total error improvement of \textbf{62.5\%} (PeMS-BAY) and \textbf{68.4\%} (METR-LA).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Multi-horizon Temporal Forecasting}
To test the model's utility for real-time traffic management, predictions were evaluated at three critical look-ahead horizons.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Multi-horizon Forecasting Performance (MAPE \%)}
    \label{tab:forecasting}
    \begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Dataset} & \textbf{15 min} & \textbf{30 min} & \textbf{60 min} \\ \midrule
        PeMS-BAY (Highway) & 3.88\% & 5.42\%* & 7.80\% \\
        METR-LA (Urban)    & 5.12\%* & 8.15\%* & 11.02\% \\ \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \small 
\end{table}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Short-term (15 min):} PeMS-BAY maintained a MAPE of \textbf{3.88\%}, demonstrating that highway speeds are highly predictable.
    \item \textbf{Mid-to-Long-term (30--60 min):} As the horizon expanded, MAPE increased to \textbf{7.80\%} (PeMS-BAY) and \textbf{11.02\%} (METR-LA).
    \item \textbf{Reliability:} All MAPE values remained below the \textbf{12\%} threshold, confirming the LSTM's ability to capture long-range dependencies without vanishing gradients.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Geographic Fairness and Client-Level Analysis}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Highway Consistency (PeMS-BAY):} Performance was uniform with a Coefficient of Variation (CV) of only \textbf{2.8\%}.
    \item \textbf{Urban Heterogeneity (METR-LA):} Client-0 (Downtown) achieved high accuracy (MAE 2.31), whereas Client-2 (Port Area) faced higher errors (MAE 3.15) due to heavy-vehicle movements.
    \item \textbf{Collaborative Boost:} The Port Area client stabilized its MAPE at \textbf{8.91\%} by "borrowing" structural logic from stable nodes via FL.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Privacy-Performance Trade-off}
To validate the effectiveness of the proposed framework, the Federated Learning (FL) model was benchmarked against a \textbf{Centralized Baseline} (the "Oracle" scenario), where all historical traffic data is aggregated on a single server for training.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy Preservation:} The FL model successfully retained approximately \textbf{91.5\% to 92\%} of the accuracy achieved by the centralized counterpart. The minor absolute increase in MAPE (approx. 0.6\% to 0.92\%) is categorized as a negligible \textit{"Privacy Tax"}---a small cost for the substantial security gains provided.
    
    \item \textbf{Root Causes of the Performance Gap:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textit{Communication Constraints:} Unlike centralized training, FedAvg relies on discretized aggregation at fixed communication rounds.
        \item \textit{Data Non-IID Effects:} Statistical heterogeneity across edge nodes (e.g., complex port area patterns) creates a residual disadvantage that global averaging cannot fully eliminate.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Systemic Efficiency and Regulatory Compliance:}
    \begin{description}
        \item[Data Privacy:] Raw sensor data never leaves the origin point, ensuring compliance with global regulations such as \textbf{GDPR} and \textbf{CCPA}.
        \item[Bandwidth Optimization:] The framework achieved a transformative \textbf{262x reduction} in communication overhead by transmitting only model weights.
        \item[Edge Readiness:] With an optimized model size of \textbf{20 KB}, the architecture enables local inference latencies \textbf{below 100ms}.
    \end{description}
\end{itemize}

\textit{Operational Conclusion:} For Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS), where signal control typically tolerates a $\pm$5--10\% error margin, the FL model's performance is functionally equivalent to centralized systems while offering superior operational resilience.
