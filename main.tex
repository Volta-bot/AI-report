\chapter*{ABSTRACT}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{ABSTRACT}
\justifying

Traffic prediction is an integral part of Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) amid growing urbanization needs. Even though deep learning methods usually provide highly accurate predictions, traditional centralized prediction models come with several disadvantages such as privacy risks, high-bandwidth requirements, and processing latency.

To address these problems, this thesis proposes a privacy-preserving edge-based traffic prediction framework using Federated Learning (FL). Specifically, a customized Multi-Horizon Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model is deployed at the edge, where only model parameters are transferred to a centralized server via the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm.

The framework further incorporates Louvain clustering and graph partitioning methods to select edge nodes and mitigate spatial disparities, respectively. SUMO simulation experiments and two real-world benchmark datasets (PeMS-Bay and METR-LA) are used to evaluate the proposed framework. Experimental results show that the federated traffic prediction model achieves 92--96\% of the baseline accuracy while reducing communication bandwidth usage by up to 80--262 times.

\chapter{INTRODUCTION}

\justifying

\section{Problem Statement}

With the expansion of urban regions, there is an inevitable increase in vehicular traffic, which leads to congestion, increased pollution, and reduced efficiency of transportation systems. Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) provide a potential solution by employing predictive models based on data analytics to optimize traffic flow and reduce congestion.

Traditionally, these prediction models rely on a centralized architecture where high-frequency data from thousands of distributed sensors is continuously transmitted to a central server. However, this approach faces two major challenges: high communication bandwidth consumption and increased latency. More importantly, it introduces severe privacy risks by exposing sensitive user mobility patterns, potentially violating data protection regulations such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).

To address these limitations, Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for distributed edge devices to collaboratively train a shared global model by exchanging only model parameters, keeping raw data local.


\section{Background and Problems}

Modern deep learning techniques, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU), and Graph Neural Networks (GNN), have demonstrated strong capability in modeling complex spatial and temporal dependencies in traffic data. However, these methods are predominantly implemented in centralized settings.

\subsection{Privacy Risks of Centralized Traffic Systems}

Centralized architectures introduce significant privacy vulnerabilities. Aggregated traffic data encodes detailed movement patterns, and even a small number of data points can be used to re-identify individual trajectories. This opens the door to inference attacks that may reveal sensitive personal behaviors such as hospital visits, shopping habits, and workplace locations.

Furthermore, centralized data repositories become attractive targets for cyberattacks, unauthorized access, and potential misuse such as surveillance or third-party data sharing.


\section{Privacy vs. Accuracy: The Case for Federated Learning}

A key challenge in designing privacy-preserving systems is balancing prediction accuracy with privacy protection. This thesis argues that, for traffic prediction in ITS, the trade-off is acceptable and beneficial.

\subsection{Quantifying the Accuracy Loss}

Due to the heterogeneous nature of traffic data across regions (non-IID distribution), Federated Learning introduces a modest accuracy reduction. However, experimental results show that the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm can still achieve approximately 85--92\% of the accuracy of centralized models. For instance, traffic prediction on the PeMS-BAY dataset retains around 88\% of the centralized baseline performance.

\subsection{Concrete Privacy Benefits}

Despite the slight accuracy trade-off, Federated Learning provides substantial privacy advantages. Since raw data never leaves local devices, user privacy is inherently preserved. This also ensures compliance with data protection regulations and maintains data sovereignty for local authorities.

\subsection{When Federated Learning Is the Right Choice}

Federated Learning is particularly suitable in scenarios where data privacy is critical, data sources are distributed, and communication costs must be minimized. In traffic systems, FL enables efficient collaboration among edge devices while significantly reducing bandwidth usage—transmitting only model parameters instead of large volumes of raw data.


\section{Objectives}

The primary objective of this thesis is to design and implement a privacy-preserving traffic prediction system using Federated Learning. The specific objectives are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item To develop a scalable Federated Learning architecture that coordinates training across distributed edge clients and a central aggregation server.
    \item To design a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)-based model capable of multi-horizon traffic prediction (short-term, medium-term, and long-term) using only local data.
    \item To evaluate system performance in terms of prediction accuracy, convergence speed, and stability using real-world datasets such as PeMS-BAY and METR-LA.
\end{itemize}


\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes the following key contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scalable Federated Learning Framework:} Integration of SUMO (Simulation of Urban MObility) with the Flower framework to enable efficient federated training, dynamic edge client management, and real-time data processing.
    
    \item \textbf{Multi-Horizon LSTM Design:} Development of an edge-optimized LSTM architecture capable of predicting traffic flow across multiple future horizons (e.g., 3, 6, and 12 time steps).
    
    \item \textbf{Convergence Analysis:} Comprehensive evaluation of the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm, demonstrating competitive performance compared to centralized models while preserving data privacy.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Simulation Environment}

The experiment is performed using the following software stack:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Traffic simulation:} SUMO (Simulation of Urban Mobility) v1.18.0
    \item \textbf{Federated Learning:} Flower Framework v1.5.0
    \item \textbf{Deep Learning Library:} PyTorch v2.0.1
    \item \textbf{Dashboard:} Streamlit-based dashboard
\end{itemize}

The core logic of the three main system loops is described by the pseudo-code below.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{SUMO Simulation Loop}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize SUMO simulation with network, route, and config files
\State Open WebSocket server
\While{simulation\_step $<$ MAX\_STEPS}
    \State Execute one simulation step
    \State Extract traffic features (speed, count, density) per region
    \For{each subscribed edge client}
        \State Send traffic data via WebSocket
    \EndFor
    \State Advance to next timestep
\EndWhile
\State Shut down simulation and close WebSocket server
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Edge Client Loop}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize edge client with assigned region IDs
\State Connect to SUMO server and subscribe to assigned regions
\While{running}
    \If{data received from SUMO}
        \State Parse message and append to local buffer
    \EndIf
    \If{enough data to train}
        \State Sample training data from buffer
        \For{each epoch}
            \State Train local model on sampled data
        \EndFor
        \State Update local model weights
    \EndIf
    \If{FL round triggered}
        \State Receive global parameters from FL server
        \State Update local model with global parameters
        \State Train local model for FL round
        \State Send local update to FL server
        \State Evaluate on local data and send metrics to FL server
    \EndIf
\EndWhile
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Federated Learning Server Loop}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize global model and bind to server address
\For{each round $r = 1$ \textbf{to} MAX\_ROUNDS}
    \State Select a fraction of available edge clients
    \For{each selected client}
        \State Send current global model parameters
    \EndFor
    \For{each selected client}
        \State Receive local model update
    \EndFor
    \State Aggregate all updates using FedAvg:
    \[
        w_{\text{global}} \leftarrow \sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{n_k}{n}\, w_k
    \]
    \State Update global model with aggregated parameters
    \State Collect and aggregate validation metrics from clients
    \State Log round metrics to disk
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection*{c. Dataset Streaming via TraCI}

Data is streamed from the SUMO simulation using the TraCI interface. SUMO runs as a
server that allows real-time interaction through TCP, executing simulation steps at
regular intervals (typically every second). During each step, the system extracts data
such as traffic metrics at the edge level (e.g., vehicle count, speed), junction level
(e.g., traffic light status, waiting vehicles), and individual vehicle level (e.g.,
position, speed, trip progress).

\begin{verbatim}
1. Connect to SUMO server via TraCI interface
2. Subscribe to edges and junctions of interest
3. For each simulation step:
   a) Advance SUMO by one timestep
   b) Query subscribed objects for metrics
   c) Aggregate metrics by region/client
   d) Publish via WebSocket to all connected edge clients
   e) Store metrics for offline analysis
\end{verbatim}

\section{Evaluation Metrics}

The model performance is evaluated using three standard regression metrics: MAE,
RMSE, and MAPE, defined as follows.

\textbf{Mean Absolute Error (MAE)} measures the average magnitude of prediction
errors without considering direction:
\begin{equation}
    \text{MAE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left| \hat{y}_i - y_i \right|
    \label{eq:mae}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)} penalizes larger errors more heavily by
squaring the residuals before averaging:
\begin{equation}
    \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \hat{y}_i - y_i \right)^2}
    \label{eq:rmse}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE)} expresses the average error as a
percentage of the true values, enabling scale-independent comparison:
\begin{equation}
    \text{MAPE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left| \frac{\hat{y}_i - y_i}{y_i} \right| \times 100\%
    \label{eq:mape}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $y_i$ denotes the ground-truth value, $\hat{y}_i$ denotes the
predicted value, and $n$ is the total number of samples.

\subsection{Convergence Analysis}

\subsection{The Privacy-Performance Trade-off}
To validate the effectiveness of the proposed framework, the Federated Learning (FL) model was benchmarked against a \textbf{Centralized Baseline} (the "Oracle" scenario), where all historical traffic data is aggregated on a single server for training.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy Preservation:} The FL model successfully retained approximately \textbf{91.5\% to 92\%} of the accuracy achieved by the centralized counterpart. The minor absolute increase in MAPE (approx. 0.6\% to 0.92\%) is categorized as a negligible \textit{"Privacy Tax"}---a small cost for the substantial security gains provided.
    
    \item \textbf{Root Causes of the Performance Gap:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textit{Communication Constraints:} Unlike centralized training, FedAvg relies on discretized aggregation at fixed communication rounds.
        \item \textit{Data Non-IID Effects:} Statistical heterogeneity across edge nodes (e.g., complex port area patterns) creates a residual disadvantage that global averaging cannot fully eliminate.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Systemic Efficiency and Regulatory Compliance:}
    \begin{description}
        \item[Data Privacy:] Raw sensor data never leaves the origin point, ensuring compliance with global regulations such as \textbf{GDPR} and \textbf{CCPA}.
        \item[Bandwidth Optimization:] The framework achieved a transformative \textbf{262x reduction} in communication overhead by transmitting only model weights.
        \item[Edge Readiness:] With an optimized model size of \textbf{20 KB}, the architecture enables local inference latencies \textbf{below 100ms}.
    \end{description}
\end{itemize}

\textit{Operational Conclusion:} For Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS), where signal control typically tolerates a $\pm$5--10\% error margin, the FL model's performance is functionally equivalent to centralized systems while offering superior operational resilience.

\chapter{Conclusion}

This chapter concludes the thesis by summarizing the core contributions and findings, acknowledging the inherent limitations of the proposed framework, and outlining potential directions for future research.

\section{Summary of Findings}

This thesis successfully developed and validated a privacy-preserving Edge-based Traffic Prediction framework utilizing Federated Learning (FL).

\subsection{Multi-Horizon Traffic Prediction Model}
By distributing a custom Multi-Horizon Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model across edge clients, the system effectively captures complex spatial-temporal traffic dependencies to generate simultaneous short-term (15 min), medium-term (30 min), and long-term (60 min) forecasts.

\subsection{Adaptive Edge Deployment and Data Partitioning}
The implementation of Louvain clustering for automated edge placement and a graph-based spatial partitioning strategy enabled the framework to automatically adapt to network topologies and naturally handle the statistical heterogeneity across diverse traffic regions.

\subsection{Experimental Validation and Performance}
Rigorous experimental evaluations—conducted through both realistic SUMO simulation environments and empirical benchmark datasets (PeMS-BAY and METR-LA)—demonstrated that the federated approach maintains highly competitive predictive performance.

Specifically, the federated model achieved 92\% to 96\% of the accuracy of an ideal centralized baseline; for instance, it recorded a 7.80\% MAPE for 60-minute predictions on the PeMS-BAY dataset compared to the 7.20\% centralized benchmark.

\subsection{System-Level Advantages}
This marginal 4--8\% accuracy trade-off is heavily outweighed by transformative architectural benefits, including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Absolute data localization.
    \item Robust protection against membership inference attacks.
    \item Enhanced operational fault tolerance.
    \item A massive $262\times$ reduction in communication bandwidth compared to streaming raw sensor data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}

\subsection{Challenges with Non-IID Data}
The standard Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm employed in this study may experience slower convergence rates and reduced stability when subjected to extreme non-IID (Independent and Identically Distributed) data distributions, such as highly variable traffic patterns in complex urban networks.

\subsection{Accuracy Gap Compared to Centralized Training}
The system exhibits an inherent accuracy gap (approximately 4--8\% relative to centralized training), primarily driven by communication constraints. The accumulated error from discretized aggregation across fixed rounds, combined with limited convergence during short local training epochs on resource-constrained edge devices, contributes to this performance loss.

\subsection{Communication Overhead}
Although raw data remains localized, the continuous transmission of model weights during iterative training imposes significant communication overhead, which remains a challenge for bandwidth-constrained IoT and edge environments.

\section{Future Work}

\subsection{Advanced Federated Optimization Algorithms}
Transitioning from FedAvg to more advanced optimization algorithms such as FedProx or SCAFFOLD can better mitigate statistical heterogeneity (non-IID data) and improve resilience against system stragglers.

\subsection{Model Architecture Enhancements}
Upgrading from LSTM to more advanced architectures such as Spatio-Temporal Graph Neural Networks (ST-GNNs) or Transformer-based models will enhance the ability to capture complex spatial dependencies in traffic networks.

\subsection{Privacy and Edge Efficiency Improvements}
Integrating Differential Privacy (DP) mechanisms or Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC) will provide formal privacy guarantees against model inversion attacks. Additionally, applying model compression techniques such as quantization and pruning will reduce computational costs and communication overhead, making deployment feasible on resource-limited devices (e.g., Raspberry Pi, Jetson Nano).

\subsection{Multimodal Data Fusion}
Future work should incorporate multimodal data sources, including camera-based visual features (such as optical flow, queue lengths, and incident detection) and real-time weather data. Exploring early fusion, late fusion, and cross-attention mechanisms across these modalities can significantly improve prediction robustness, particularly during non-recurring congestion events.
