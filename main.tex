\chapter*{ABSTRACT}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{ABSTRACT}
\justifying

Traffic prediction is an integral part of Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) amid growing urbanization needs. Even though deep learning methods usually provide highly accurate predictions, traditional centralized prediction models come with several disadvantages such as privacy risks, high-bandwidth requirements, and processing latency.

To address these problems, this thesis proposes a privacy-preserving edge-based traffic prediction framework using Federated Learning (FL). Specifically, a customized Multi-Horizon Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model is deployed at the edge, where only model parameters are transferred to a centralized server via the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm.

The framework further incorporates Louvain clustering and graph partitioning methods to select edge nodes and mitigate spatial disparities, respectively. SUMO simulation experiments and two real-world benchmark datasets (PeMS-Bay and METR-LA) are used to evaluate the proposed framework. Experimental results show that the federated traffic prediction model achieves 92--96\% of the baseline accuracy while reducing communication bandwidth usage by up to 80--262 times.

\chapter{INTRODUCTION}

\justifying

\section{Problem Statement}

With the expansion of urban regions, there is an inevitable increase in vehicular traffic, which leads to congestion, increased pollution, and reduced efficiency of transportation systems. Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) provide a potential solution by employing predictive models based on data analytics to optimize traffic flow and reduce congestion.

Traditionally, these prediction models rely on a centralized architecture where high-frequency data from thousands of distributed sensors is continuously transmitted to a central server. However, this approach faces two major challenges: high communication bandwidth consumption and increased latency. More importantly, it introduces severe privacy risks by exposing sensitive user mobility patterns, potentially violating data protection regulations such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).

To address these limitations, Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for distributed edge devices to collaboratively train a shared global model by exchanging only model parameters, keeping raw data local.


\section{Background and Problems}

Modern deep learning techniques, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU), and Graph Neural Networks (GNN), have demonstrated strong capability in modeling complex spatial and temporal dependencies in traffic data. However, these methods are predominantly implemented in centralized settings.

\subsection{Privacy Risks of Centralized Traffic Systems}

Centralized architectures introduce significant privacy vulnerabilities. Aggregated traffic data encodes detailed movement patterns, and even a small number of data points can be used to re-identify individual trajectories. This opens the door to inference attacks that may reveal sensitive personal behaviors such as hospital visits, shopping habits, and workplace locations.

Furthermore, centralized data repositories become attractive targets for cyberattacks, unauthorized access, and potential misuse such as surveillance or third-party data sharing.


\section{Privacy vs. Accuracy: The Case for Federated Learning}

A key challenge in designing privacy-preserving systems is balancing prediction accuracy with privacy protection. This thesis argues that, for traffic prediction in ITS, the trade-off is acceptable and beneficial.

\subsection{Quantifying the Accuracy Loss}

Due to the heterogeneous nature of traffic data across regions (non-IID distribution), Federated Learning introduces a modest accuracy reduction. However, experimental results show that the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm can still achieve approximately 85--92\% of the accuracy of centralized models. For instance, traffic prediction on the PeMS-BAY dataset retains around 88\% of the centralized baseline performance.

\subsection{Concrete Privacy Benefits}

Despite the slight accuracy trade-off, Federated Learning provides substantial privacy advantages. Since raw data never leaves local devices, user privacy is inherently preserved. This also ensures compliance with data protection regulations and maintains data sovereignty for local authorities.

\subsection{When Federated Learning Is the Right Choice}

Federated Learning is particularly suitable in scenarios where data privacy is critical, data sources are distributed, and communication costs must be minimized. In traffic systems, FL enables efficient collaboration among edge devices while significantly reducing bandwidth usage—transmitting only model parameters instead of large volumes of raw data.


\section{Objectives}

The primary objective of this thesis is to design and implement a privacy-preserving traffic prediction system using Federated Learning. The specific objectives are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item To develop a scalable Federated Learning architecture that coordinates training across distributed edge clients and a central aggregation server.
    \item To design a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)-based model capable of multi-horizon traffic prediction (short-term, medium-term, and long-term) using only local data.
    \item To evaluate system performance in terms of prediction accuracy, convergence speed, and stability using real-world datasets such as PeMS-BAY and METR-LA.
\end{itemize}


\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes the following key contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scalable Federated Learning Framework:} Integration of SUMO (Simulation of Urban MObility) with the Flower framework to enable efficient federated training, dynamic edge client management, and real-time data processing.
    
    \item \textbf{Multi-Horizon LSTM Design:} Development of an edge-optimized LSTM architecture capable of predicting traffic flow across multiple future horizons (e.g., 3, 6, and 12 time steps).
    
    \item \textbf{Convergence Analysis:} Comprehensive evaluation of the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm, demonstrating competitive performance compared to centralized models while preserving data privacy.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{REQUIREMENT SURVEY AND ANALYSIS}

This chapter provides a comprehensive review of the existing literature related to traffic flow prediction, the emergence of Federated Learning (FL) in Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS), and the specific application of FL for privacy-preserving traffic forecasting. Furthermore, it presents the theoretical background of the core technologies used in this thesis, namely Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm.

\section{Traffic Flow Prediction}
Traffic flow prediction is a fundamental component of modern ITS. Accurate forecasting enables traffic management centers to mitigate congestion, optimize signal timing, and provide real-time route guidance to drivers.

\subsection{Traditional Approaches}
Early approaches to traffic prediction relied heavily on statistical methods. The Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model and its variants (e.g., SARIMA, KARIMA) were widely used due to their mathematical soundness. However, these linear models often struggle to capture the complex, non-linear, and dynamic spatiotemporal dependencies inherent in traffic data. Kalman Filters have also been employed for real-time estimation but typically require strict assumptions about the state-space model.

\subsection{Deep Learning Approaches}
The advent of Deep Learning has revolutionized traffic prediction. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), and specifically Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs), have become the standard for modeling temporal dependencies in time-series data. These models can effectively learn long-term patterns and handle non-linear relationships.

More recently, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have been introduced to explicitly model the spatial topology of road networks. Approaches like Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) combined with temporal modules (T-GCN, ST-GCN) have achieved state-of-the-art performance by simultaneously capturing spatial correlations between road segments and temporal trends. However, these centralized deep learning models require aggregating massive amounts of data at a central server, raising significant privacy and bandwidth concerns.

\section{Federated Learning in Intelligent Transportation Systems}
With the development of intelligent transportation systems, the scale and complexity of traffic data are continuously increasing, and privacy and security concerns are also gaining more attention. In this context, federated learning, as a distributed machine learning paradigm, can effectively address data privacy and security issues in the transportation domain while enhancing the efficiency of traffic prediction and management.

In 2017, the classic federated averaging algorithm was introduced. McMahan proposed a federated learning algorithm based on gradients, discussing how to address data security and privacy issues in a distributed environment, laying an important foundation for the research and application of federated learning. Since 2018, scholars have found that data imbalance and other issues often occur in practical applications. In order to improve the efficiency and scalability of federated learning, Zhao et al. proposed a weighted average based federated learning algorithm. This algorithm adds a weight to the local model updates of each client to balance the impact between different clients, significantly improving accuracy.

In 2020, Wei et al. proposed a federated learning algorithm that combines differential privacy protection, introducing a noise addition mechanism applied to model updating and aggregation. In 2021, Qi et al. proposed a federated learning algorithm based on differential privacy to improve the accuracy of traffic prediction while protecting data privacy. Trustworthy FL is becoming critical for critical infrastructure like ITS, enhancing security and communication efficiency across Internet of Vehicles (IoV) and edge computing environments where data is naturally distributed and sensitive.

\section{State-of-the-Art in Federated Traffic Prediction}
Recent advancements in Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) have heavily adopted federated learning to balance the need for accurate traffic prediction with strict data privacy constraints. Current state-of-the-art research addresses three primary challenges: capturing complex spatial-temporal dependencies across distributed nodes, managing data heterogeneity among clients, and ensuring robust system security. 

\subsection{Spatial-Temporal Modeling in FL}
A major challenge in federated traffic forecasting is accurately modeling the complex spatial and temporal correlations without centralizing the data. Wang et al. proposed FedSTM, a privacy-preserving spatiotemporal flow prediction model inspired by high-definition image generation techniques. Their approach utilizes a fine-grained Traffic State Pixel Embedding method to quantify and extract atomic-level features from both spatiotemporal and semantic dimensions. This successfully captures highly complex traffic dynamics across distributed networks. However, treating traffic states like high-definition image frames can introduce substantial computational overhead for resource-constrained edge devices. In contrast, our proposed methodology utilizes a highly optimized, lightweight Multi-Horizon LSTM architecture that achieves temporal accuracy with only 5,000 parameters, ensuring it remains feasible for standard IoT edge nodes.

\subsection{Heterogeneity and Personalization}
In real-world ITS deployments, data collected across different sensors is inherently non-IID (Independent and Identically Distributed), suffering from varying volumes and regional characteristics. Li et al. proposed a communication-efficient federated learning method specifically designed for traffic flow prediction to address these deployment bottlenecks. Their framework successfully reduces communication delays and handles the uneven distribution of data across distributed sensors. While this improves global communication efficiency, a single global model often struggles to perfectly personalize to completely distinct urban topologies (e.g., highway vs. arterial roads). Our system bridges this gap by introducing a graph-based, topology-aware edge placement strategy using Louvain clustering, which naturally groups structurally similar traffic communities to ensure regional relevance and fairness.

\subsection{Privacy and Security Enhancements}
While federated learning inherently minimizes raw data exposure, the quality of local data and network stability can compromise system robustness and security. Cheng et al. proposed a federated contrastive learning approach aimed at building robust traffic classifiers under low-quality data conditions. Their framework specifically tackles realistic network flaws, such as packet loss and skewed data distributions, successfully maintaining model integrity and learning robust representations without relying on isolated, perfect network simulations. While their work focuses heavily on network traffic classification, our methodology applies similar principles of asynchronous communication and robustness to the domain of multi-horizon traffic speed prediction, utilizing a 300-second per-client timeout and exponential backoff to ensure reliable aggregation even when edge nodes face connectivity issues.

\section{Theoretical Background}

\subsection{Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)}
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are a special kind of RNN capable of learning long-term dependencies. They were introduced to solve the vanishing gradient problem of traditional RNNs. An LSTM unit is composed of a cell, an input gate, an output gate, and a forget gate. The cell remembers values over arbitrary time intervals and the three gates regulate the flow of information into and out of the cell.

For a given input sequence $x=(x_{1},x_{2},...,x_{T})$, the LSTM computes the hidden state sequence $h=(h_{1},h_{2},...,h_{T})$ by iterating the following equations from $t=1$ to $T$:

\begin{equation}
f_{t}=\sigma(W_{f}\cdot[h_{t-1},x_{t}]+b_{f})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
i_{t}=\sigma(W_{i}\cdot[h_{t-1},x_{t}]+b_{i})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\overline{C}_{t}=\tanh(W_{C}\cdot[h_{t-1},x_{t}]+b_{C})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
C_{t}=f_{t}*C_{t-1}+i_{t}*\overline{C}_{t}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
o_{t}=\sigma(W_{o}\cdot[h_{t-1},x_{t}]+b_{o})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
h_{t}=o_{t}*\tanh(C_{t})
\end{equation}

where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function, $*$ denotes the Hadamard product, and $W$ and $b$ are weight matrices and bias vectors, respectively.

\subsection{Federated Averaging (FedAvg)}
Federated Averaging (FedAvg) is a technique used to train machine learning models where data is spread across many different servers or devices. It ensures data privacy and security and maintains data locality by enabling model training without sharing the raw data.

In the conventional approach of centralized machine learning, data is collected and stored in a central server, and a single model is trained on this consolidated data. Conversely, in federated averaging, data remains decentralized, residing on numerous devices or edge devices, and the model's training is conducted locally on each specific device. The goal is to minimize the global loss function $F(w)$:

\begin{equation}
\min_{w}F(w)=\sum_{k=1}^{K}\frac{n_{k}}{n}F_{k}(w)
\end{equation}

where $K$ is the total number of clients, $n_{k}$ is the number of samples on client $k$, $n$ is the total number of samples, and $F_{k}(w)$ is the local loss function of client $k$.

In each round $t$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The server selects a subset of clients $S_{t}$.
    \item The server sends the current global model $w_{t}$ to these clients.
    \item Each client $k$ performs $E$ epochs of local training (SGD) to obtain $w_{t+1}^{k}$.
    \item The server aggregates the updates:
\end{enumerate}

\begin{equation}
w_{t+1}=\sum_{k\in S_{t}}\frac{n_{k}}{n}w_{t+1}^{k}
\end{equation}

\chapter{Methodology}

\section{Overview}
Chapter 3 presents a comprehensive framework for an Edge-based Traffic Prediction system using Federated Learning (FL). The system is specifically designed to address the critical challenges of privacy, bandwidth, and latency in Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS). By dividing traffic regions into independent and isolated clients, the architecture ensures that raw traffic data never leaves the local environment, thereby guaranteeing robust data privacy. To overcome the challenges of insufficient or poor-quality data at individual nodes, the system leverages FL to collaboratively train a global model. Furthermore, it addresses the issue of model parameter bloat by utilizing a lightweight architecture to reduce communication burdens, while incorporating mechanisms to handle data imbalance among clients.

\section{Federated Training Process}
The framework operates on a distributed architecture consisting of two primary roles: Local Clients and a Central Server.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Local Clients:} Function as independent computing units that manage local data, train the model using parameters dispatched by the server, and upload the updated model weights.
    \item \textbf{Central Server:} Acts as the coordinator that operates completely independent of the clients' raw data. It is responsible for initializing the global model, distributing parameters, aggregating client updates, and generating the new global model for the next iteration.
\end{itemize}

\section{Collaborative Training Process}
The collaborative training process follows a strict sequence in each communication round:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Initialization \& Distribution:} The server initializes the global model weights $W_0$ and distributes the current weights $W_r$ to selected clients.
    \item \textbf{Local Training:} Each client $k$ uses its private local dataset $D_k$ to train the model and minimize prediction error.
    \item \textbf{Upload \& Aggregation:} Clients upload their updated weights $W_r^k$ to the server, which aggregates them using the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm to form the new global model $W_{r+1}$.
    \item \textbf{Iteration:} This process repeats for a fixed number of rounds $R$ or until convergence is achieved.
\end{itemize}

\section{System Architecture and Components}
The system is built upon five integrated subsystems designed to support both simulated and real-world data environments.

\subsection{Traffic Data Environment}
The environment operates in two distinct modes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Simulation Mode:} Utilizes the SUMO (Simulation of Urban MObility) platform to generate realistic, multi-modal traffic flows on road networks imported from OpenStreetMap.
    \item \textbf{Real-World Mode:} Integrates benchmark datasets (PEMS-BAY and METR-LA) with 5-minute temporal resolutions for rigorous validation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Streaming Layer}
Real-time data flows via a WebSocket-based publish-subscribe architecture. A Python server publishes high-frequency traffic metrics (speed, vehicle count, occupancy) to edge clients in structured JSON messages. Each client maintains a fixed-size circular buffer storing 300 seconds of aggregated data to ensure continuous, memory-efficient training.

\subsection{Edge Clients}
Edge Clients maintain persistent WebSocket connections, buffer data, and perform local training using PyTorch with the Adam optimizer (learning rate 0.001, batch size 32). They utilize the Flower framework interface to communicate seamlessly with the server.

\subsection{Federated Learning Server}
Also utilizing the Flower framework, the server coordinates training with configurations such as a minimum of 5 clients per round and a 300-second timeout window. It evaluates the aggregated model against held-out test data after each round to track convergence.

\subsection{Multi-Horizon LSTM Architecture}
To predict future traffic sequences based on historical observations, the system employs a highly optimized Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Structure:} The encoder consists of two stacked LSTM layers with 64 units each.
    \item \textbf{Multi-Head Output:} Instead of a single output, the model features three distinct fully connected heads to simultaneously predict short-term (15 minutes), medium-term (30 minutes), and long-term (60 minutes) traffic conditions.
    \item \textbf{Efficiency:} The model is exceptionally lightweight, containing only 5,000 parameters and a weight file size of 20 KB. This ensures it fits comfortably on edge devices and requires only 12-15 seconds of training time per round on a standard CPU.
\end{itemize}

\section{Network Topology Analysis for Edge Placement}
A crucial innovation in this methodology is the automated, topology-based placement of edge clients within the road network, avoiding arbitrary manual configurations.

\subsection{Louvain Clustering for Community Detection}
The system constructs an undirected graph of the road network and applies Louvain clustering to partition the network into 4-10 natural traffic communities by optimizing modularity.

\subsection{Centrality-Based Representative Selection}
Within each community, the optimal edge location is determined by calculating a combined score of Betweenness Centrality (measuring how often a segment is on the shortest path) and Closeness Centrality (measuring the average distance to other segments).

\subsection{Benefits of Topology-Based Placement}
This spatial partitioning strategy ensures balanced regional fairness, accurately mimics the coverage of physical Roadside Units (RSUs), and successfully introduces the statistical heterogeneity necessary to test the FL algorithm.

\section{Federated Aggregation Algorithm}
The server aggregates local models using a weighted FedAvg algorithm, mathematically defined as
$$W_{r+1} = \sum_{k \in S_r} \frac{n_k}{n} W_r^k$$
where weights are proportional to the size of the clients' local data to ensure fair influence.

\section{Communication Protocol and Efficiency}

\subsection{Asynchronous Communication}
To handle the realities of network communication, the system uses an asynchronous protocol. It employs a 300-second per-client timeout and exponential backoff for upload failures, ensuring that the aggregation proceeds as long as a minimum threshold of clients successfully upload.

\subsection{Bandwidth Efficiency Gains}
By compressing the model to just 22 KB per client, the architecture achieves an 80-100x reduction in bandwidth costs compared to centralized raw data transmission

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Simulation Environment}

The experiment is performed using the following software stack:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Traffic simulation:} SUMO (Simulation of Urban Mobility) v1.18.0
    \item \textbf{Federated Learning:} Flower Framework v1.5.0
    \item \textbf{Deep Learning Library:} PyTorch v2.0.1
    \item \textbf{Dashboard:} Streamlit-based dashboard
\end{itemize}

The core logic of the three main system loops is described by the pseudo-code below.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{SUMO Simulation Loop}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize SUMO simulation with network, route, and config files
\State Open WebSocket server
\While{simulation\_step $<$ MAX\_STEPS}
    \State Execute one simulation step
    \State Extract traffic features (speed, count, density) per region
    \For{each subscribed edge client}
        \State Send traffic data via WebSocket
    \EndFor
    \State Advance to next timestep
\EndWhile
\State Shut down simulation and close WebSocket server
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Edge Client Loop}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize edge client with assigned region IDs
\State Connect to SUMO server and subscribe to assigned regions
\While{running}
    \If{data received from SUMO}
        \State Parse message and append to local buffer
    \EndIf
    \If{enough data to train}
        \State Sample training data from buffer
        \For{each epoch}
            \State Train local model on sampled data
        \EndFor
        \State Update local model weights
    \EndIf
    \If{FL round triggered}
        \State Receive global parameters from FL server
        \State Update local model with global parameters
        \State Train local model for FL round
        \State Send local update to FL server
        \State Evaluate on local data and send metrics to FL server
    \EndIf
\EndWhile
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Federated Learning Server Loop}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize global model and bind to server address
\For{each round $r = 1$ \textbf{to} MAX\_ROUNDS}
    \State Select a fraction of available edge clients
    \For{each selected client}
        \State Send current global model parameters
    \EndFor
    \For{each selected client}
        \State Receive local model update
    \EndFor
    \State Aggregate all updates using FedAvg:
    \[
        w_{\text{global}} \leftarrow \sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{n_k}{n}\, w_k
    \]
    \State Update global model with aggregated parameters
    \State Collect and aggregate validation metrics from clients
    \State Log round metrics to disk
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

server that allows real-time interaction through TCP, executing simulation steps at
regular intervals (typically every second). During each step, the system extracts data
such as traffic metrics at the edge level (e.g., vehicle count, speed), junction level
(e.g., traffic light status, waiting vehicles), and individual vehicle level (e.g.,
position, speed, trip progress).

\begin{verbatim}
1. Connect to SUMO server via TraCI interface
2. Subscribe to edges and junctions of interest
3. For each simulation step:
   a) Advance SUMO by one timestep
   b) Query subscribed objects for metrics
   c) Aggregate metrics by region/client
   d) Publish via WebSocket to all connected edge clients
   e) Store metrics for offline analysis
\end{verbatim}

\subsubsection*{d. WebSocket Data Publisher}

Traffic data is distributed to edge clients using a WebSocket-based publisher. The
system sends real-time JSON messages at each simulation step (1 Hz), allowing clients

\section{Evaluation Metrics}

The model performance is evaluated using three standard regression metrics: MAE,
RMSE, and MAPE, defined as follows.

\textbf{Mean Absolute Error (MAE)} measures the average magnitude of prediction
errors without considering direction:
\begin{equation}
    \text{MAE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left| \hat{y}_i - y_i \right|
    \label{eq:mae}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)} penalizes larger errors more heavily by
squaring the residuals before averaging:
\begin{equation}
    \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \hat{y}_i - y_i \right)^2}
    \label{eq:rmse}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE)} expresses the average error as a
percentage of the true values, enabling scale-independent comparison:
\begin{equation}
    \text{MAPE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left| \frac{\hat{y}_i - y_i}{y_i} \right| \times 100\%
    \label{eq:mape}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $y_i$ denotes the ground-truth value, $\hat{y}_i$ denotes the
predicted value, and $n$ is the total number of samples.

\subsection{The Privacy-Performance Trade-off}
To validate the effectiveness of the proposed framework, the Federated Learning (FL) model was benchmarked against a \textbf{Centralized Baseline} (the "Oracle" scenario), where all historical traffic data is aggregated on a single server for training.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy Preservation:} The FL model successfully retained approximately \textbf{91.5\% to 92\%} of the accuracy achieved by the centralized counterpart. The minor absolute increase in MAPE (approx. 0.6\% to 0.92\%) is categorized as a negligible \textit{"Privacy Tax"}---a small cost for the substantial security gains provided.
    
    \item \textbf{Root Causes of the Performance Gap:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textit{Communication Constraints:} Unlike centralized training, FedAvg relies on discretized aggregation at fixed communication rounds.
        \item \textit{Data Non-IID Effects:} Statistical heterogeneity across edge nodes (e.g., complex port area patterns) creates a residual disadvantage that global averaging cannot fully eliminate.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Systemic Efficiency and Regulatory Compliance:}
    \begin{description}
        \item[Data Privacy:] Raw sensor data never leaves the origin point, ensuring compliance with global regulations such as \textbf{GDPR} and \textbf{CCPA}.
        \item[Bandwidth Optimization:] The framework achieved a transformative \textbf{262x reduction} in communication overhead by transmitting only model weights.
        \item[Edge Readiness:] With an optimized model size of \textbf{20 KB}, the architecture enables local inference latencies \textbf{below 100ms}.
    \end{description}
\end{itemize}

\textit{Operational Conclusion:} For Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS), where signal control typically tolerates a $\pm$5--10\% error margin, the FL model's performance is functionally equivalent to centralized systems while offering superior operational resilience.

This chapter concludes the thesis by summarizing the core contributions and findings, acknowledging the inherent limitations of the proposed framework, and outlining potential directions for future research.

\section{Summary of Findings}

This thesis successfully developed and validated a privacy-preserving Edge-based Traffic Prediction framework utilizing Federated Learning (FL).

\subsection{Multi-Horizon Traffic Prediction Model}
By distributing a custom Multi-Horizon Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model across edge clients, the system effectively captures complex spatial-temporal traffic dependencies to generate simultaneous short-term (15 min), medium-term (30 min), and long-term (60 min) forecasts.

\subsection{Adaptive Edge Deployment and Data Partitioning}
The implementation of Louvain clustering for automated edge placement and a graph-based spatial partitioning strategy enabled the framework to automatically adapt to network topologies and naturally handle the statistical heterogeneity across diverse traffic regions.

\subsection{Experimental Validation and Performance}
Rigorous experimental evaluations—conducted through both realistic SUMO simulation environments and empirical benchmark datasets (PeMS-BAY and METR-LA)—demonstrated that the federated approach maintains highly competitive predictive performance.

Specifically, the federated model achieved 92\% to 96\% of the accuracy of an ideal centralized baseline; for instance, it recorded a 7.80\% MAPE for 60-minute predictions on the PeMS-BAY dataset compared to the 7.20\% centralized benchmark.

\subsection{System-Level Advantages}
This marginal 4--8\% accuracy trade-off is heavily outweighed by transformative architectural benefits, including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Absolute data localization.
    \item Robust protection against membership inference attacks.
    \item Enhanced operational fault tolerance.
    \item A massive $262\times$ reduction in communication bandwidth compared to streaming raw sensor data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}

\subsection{Challenges with Non-IID Data}
The standard Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm employed in this study may experience slower convergence rates and reduced stability when subjected to extreme non-IID (Independent and Identically Distributed) data distributions, such as highly variable traffic patterns in complex urban networks.

\subsection{Accuracy Gap Compared to Centralized Training}
The system exhibits an inherent accuracy gap (approximately 4--8\% relative to centralized training), primarily driven by communication constraints. The accumulated error from discretized aggregation across fixed rounds, combined with limited convergence during short local training epochs on resource-constrained edge devices, contributes to this performance loss.

\subsection{Communication Overhead}
Although raw data remains localized, the continuous transmission of model weights during iterative training imposes significant communication overhead, which remains a challenge for bandwidth-constrained IoT and edge environments.

\section{Future Work}

\subsection{Advanced Federated Optimization Algorithms}
Transitioning from FedAvg to more advanced optimization algorithms such as FedProx or SCAFFOLD can better mitigate statistical heterogeneity (non-IID data) and improve resilience against system stragglers.

\subsection{Model Architecture Enhancements}
Upgrading from LSTM to more advanced architectures such as Spatio-Temporal Graph Neural Networks (ST-GNNs) or Transformer-based models will enhance the ability to capture complex spatial dependencies in traffic networks.

\subsection{Privacy and Edge Efficiency Improvements}
Integrating Differential Privacy (DP) mechanisms or Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC) will provide formal privacy guarantees against model inversion attacks. Additionally, applying model compression techniques such as quantization and pruning will reduce computational costs and communication overhead, making deployment feasible on resource-limited devices (e.g., Raspberry Pi, Jetson Nano).

\subsection{Multimodal Data Fusion}
Future work should incorporate multimodal data sources, including camera-based visual features (such as optical flow, queue lengths, and incident detection) and real-time weather data. Exploring early fusion, late fusion, and cross-attention mechanisms across these modalities can significantly improve prediction robustness, particularly during non-recurring congestion events.
